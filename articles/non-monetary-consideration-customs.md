## Introduction

The customs valuation of imported goods is governed by the Customs Valuation (Determination of Value of Imported Goods) Rules, 2007, which implement the WTO Customs Valuation Agreement into Indian law. The primary method of valuation is the **transaction value** — the price actually paid or payable for the goods when sold for export to India.

A recurring issue in customs valuation disputes is the treatment of **non-monetary consideration** — situations where the buyer provides something of value to the seller other than money, in connection with the imported goods. This article examines how non-monetary consideration affects the determination of transaction value and the conditions under which adjustments are required.

---

## The Transaction Value Method

Rule 3 of the Customs Valuation Rules provides that the transaction value of imported goods is the price actually paid or payable for the goods, **adjusted** in accordance with Rule 10.

Rule 10 specifies the additions to be made to the price paid or payable. These include:

- Commissions and brokerage, except buying commissions
- Cost of containers treated as one for customs purposes
- Cost of packing
- Assists (tools, dies, moulds, materials, etc. supplied free or at reduced cost)
- Royalties and licence fees
- Proceeds of resale accruing to the seller
- Cost of transport, insurance, and related charges

The concept of **assists** under Rule 10(1)(b) is where non-monetary consideration most commonly arises.

---

## What Are Assists?

An "assist" is defined as any of the following supplied directly or indirectly, free of charge or at reduced cost, by the buyer for use in connection with the production or sale of the imported goods:

1. Materials, components, parts, and similar items incorporated in the imported goods
2. Tools, dies, moulds, and similar items used in the production of imported goods
3. Materials consumed in the production of imported goods
4. Engineering, development, artwork, design work, and plans and sketches undertaken outside India

Where the buyer provides any of these to the seller — at no cost or below market cost — the value of the assist must be added to the transaction value.

---

## Valuation of Assists

The Customs Valuation Rules and the accompanying interpretive notes provide guidance on how assists are to be valued:

| Type of Assist | Valuation Basis |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Purchased by buyer | Cost of acquisition |
| Produced by buyer | Cost of production |
| Previously used by buyer | Adjusted acquisition cost |
| Obtained free by buyer | Zero, but circumstances reviewed |

The apportionment of assist value across multiple shipments raises practical questions — whether the full assist value is added to a single shipment or spread across the expected volume of production.

> "The obligation to add assist value to the transaction value arises regardless of whether the assist is provided under a formal agreement or informally — substance governs over form."

---

## Non-Monetary Consideration Beyond Assists

Assists are the most common form of non-monetary consideration, but the concept extends further. Where the buyer provides **services, intellectual property, or data** to the seller in connection with the production of imported goods, the question arises whether these constitute consideration that must be reflected in the customs value.

Recent disputes have arisen in the following contexts:

### 1. Software and Data

Where a buyer provides proprietary software, algorithms, or datasets to an overseas manufacturer for use in producing customised goods, the value of such software or data may constitute non-monetary consideration. Revenue authorities have sought to include such values in the assessable value.

### 2. Marketing and Promotional Services

In some import arrangements, the buyer undertakes marketing or promotional activities in India that benefit the foreign seller's brand. Revenue authorities have argued that the cost of such services forms part of the consideration for the goods.

### 3. Deferred Payments and Offset Arrangements

In barter-like or offset arrangements — particularly in defence and infrastructure procurement — non-monetary obligations assumed by the importer may constitute consideration that affects the transaction value.

---

## Conditions for Rejection of Transaction Value

The transaction value can be rejected by customs authorities only if it falls within one of the enumerated grounds under Rule 12 — which include situations where the buyer and seller are related and the relationship has influenced the price. Non-monetary consideration that is not captured in the declared transaction value may provide a basis for rejection and re-determination under a secondary method.

---

## Conclusion

Importers must carefully assess whether their arrangements with overseas suppliers involve any form of non-monetary consideration — whether in the form of assists, services, intellectual property, or otherwise — and ensure that the assessable value declared to customs accurately reflects all elements of consideration.

Failure to do so exposes importers to show-cause notices, duty demands, and penalties under the Customs Act, 1962.
