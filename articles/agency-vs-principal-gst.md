## The Core Question

In a wide range of business arrangements — distribution networks, commission agents, consignment stockists, e-commerce operators, insurance intermediaries — a threshold question arises: is the intermediary acting as an **agent** of the principal, or is it buying and selling in its own right as a **principal**?

The answer has significant consequences under the Goods and Services Tax (GST) framework. It determines who is liable to pay tax, on what value, and at what point in the supply chain.

---

## The Statutory Framework

Under the CGST Act, 2017, Section 2(5) defines an **"agent"** as a person who supplies or receives goods or services on behalf of another, whether or not such agent discloses the name of the principal.

Schedule I to the CGST Act deems the supply of goods by a principal to an agent — where the agent is required to supply goods on behalf of the principal — to be a supply even without consideration. This deeming provision is the source of considerable litigation.

The CGST Rules further provide that:

- Where the agent issues an invoice in its own name, it is treated as the principal for GST purposes
- The value of supply in a principal-to-principal transaction is the transaction value between the two parties
- In a pure agency arrangement, the agent's supply is only the **service of agency**, not the underlying goods or services

---

## The Test: Who Bears the Risk?

The central test for distinguishing agency from principal-to-principal transactions is **who bears the economic risk of the underlying transaction**. Courts and tribunals — drawing from both pre-GST and post-GST jurisprudence — have identified the following indicators:

| Factor | Points to Agency | Points to Principal-to-Principal |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| Ownership of goods | Principal retains title | Intermediary takes title |
| Credit risk | Principal bears | Intermediary bears |
| Pricing discretion | Principal fixes price | Intermediary sets its own price |
| Return of unsold goods | Possible | Generally not |
| Invoice in whose name | Principal's name | Intermediary's own name |
| Profit model | Commission / fee | Buy-sell margin |

No single factor is determinative. The arrangement must be examined in its entirety.

---

## Key Disputes Under GST

### 1. Del Credere Agents

A **del credere agent** guarantees payment by the buyer to the principal. Revenue authorities have argued that a del credere agent, by assuming credit risk, steps out of pure agency and becomes a principal. Several Advance Ruling Authorities have taken this position.

However, the better view — supported by pre-GST authorities and the nature of del credere arrangements — is that the guarantee of payment does not change the fundamental character of the relationship as one of agency.

### 2. Consignment Stockists

Pharmaceutical and FMCG consignment stockists have been subject to intense scrutiny. Where the stockist does not bear price risk, returns unsold goods, and operates on a fixed commission, the transaction is one of agency. However, where the stockist purchases goods outright and resells, it is a principal-to-principal arrangement.

> "The label given to an arrangement by the parties is not determinative. Substance over form governs the characterisation for GST purposes."

### 3. E-Commerce Operators

Under Section 9(5) of the CGST Act, e-commerce operators are liable to pay GST on specified categories of services (restaurant services, transport of passengers, housekeeping). This is an exception to the general rule and does not automatically make an e-commerce operator a principal in other transactions.

---

## Practical Implications

The characterisation of a transaction has direct compliance implications:

1. **Input Tax Credit chain**: In a principal-to-principal arrangement, each party in the chain issues its own tax invoice and claims ITC independently. In an agency arrangement, the principal's supply is the taxable event.

2. **Valuation**: In an agency arrangement, the agent's taxable supply is only the agency service (commission). In a principal-to-principal arrangement, the full transaction value is taxable in the hands of the intermediary.

3. **Registration**: An agent acting on behalf of a principal in a state where the principal is not registered may trigger registration requirements.

---

## Conclusion

The agency versus principal-to-principal characterisation is one of the most fact-intensive enquiries in GST practice. Businesses should periodically review their distribution and intermediary arrangements to ensure that the contractual structure, commercial reality, and GST treatment are aligned.
