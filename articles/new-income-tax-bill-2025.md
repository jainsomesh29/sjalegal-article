## Overview

The New Income Tax Bill, 2025 represents the most significant structural overhaul of India's direct tax legislation since the enactment of the Income Tax Act, 1961. Introduced in Parliament on 13 February 2025, the Bill seeks to consolidate, simplify, and rationalise the existing provisions of the 1961 Act, which has grown unwieldy over six decades of amendments, judicial interpretation, and legislative accretion.

This article examines the key structural changes proposed, the implications for taxpayers and practitioners, and the areas where the Bill's drafting raises questions that will require further clarification.

---

## Background: Why a New Code?

The 1961 Act, in its current form, runs to over 800 sections, 23 schedules, and thousands of sub-provisions layered through Finance Acts over the years. The Direct Taxes Code exercise initiated in 2009 was abandoned after extensive commentary. The 2025 Bill takes a different approach — it does not seek to alter the substantive law significantly, but rather to restate it in plain language and a logical structure.

The stated objectives of the Bill are:

- Reduction in the length of the statute by eliminating redundant provisions
- Use of plain language and defined terms to reduce interpretational disputes
- Consolidation of scattered provisions relating to a single subject
- Introduction of "tax year" as a uniform concept replacing the assessment year / previous year distinction

---

## Key Structural Changes

### 1. Tax Year Replaces Assessment Year

One of the most practically significant changes is the replacement of the "previous year" and "assessment year" framework with a single unified concept of a **"tax year"**. Under the 1961 Act, income earned in the previous year is assessed in the assessment year — a distinction that has been a persistent source of confusion for taxpayers and a technical ground for litigation.

The Bill proposes that income earned in a tax year will be assessed and taxed in that same year, with the tax year corresponding to the financial year (1 April to 31 March).

### 2. Consolidation of Salary Provisions

The provisions relating to computation of income under the head "Salaries" have been consolidated and restated. The Bill brings together provisions currently spread across Sections 15 to 17 and various sub-sections in a more readable format. However, the substantive law on perquisites, allowances, and deductions remains largely unchanged.

### 3. Rationalisation of Capital Gains

The capital gains provisions — historically among the most litigated in the 1961 Act — have been reorganised. The Bill proposes a cleaner separation between:

- **Short-term capital gains** taxable at normal slab rates or special rates
- **Long-term capital gains** with and without indexation benefit
- **Deemed transfer** provisions, which have been restated with greater clarity

### 4. TDS Framework

The TDS provisions, currently scattered across Sections 192 to 206AA, have been consolidated into a dedicated chapter. The Bill introduces a table-based structure for TDS rates, which should make compliance simpler and reduce errors arising from the existing section-by-section approach.

## Areas Requiring Clarification

While the Bill is a welcome exercise in legislative housekeeping, several provisions raise interpretational questions:

> "The simplification of language should not inadvertently alter the settled legal position on complex issues where decades of judicial interpretation have created a workable framework."

1. **Carried forward losses**: The restated provisions on set-off and carry forward of losses should be tested against scenarios where the 1961 Act's provisions have been interpreted broadly by tribunals.

2. **International tax provisions**: The DTAA override provision and the treatment of Permanent Establishment income require careful reading against India's treaty network.

3. **Transitional provisions**: The Bill's approach to pending assessments, appeals, and proceedings under the 1961 Act will need to be clearly specified to avoid jurisdictional uncertainty.

---

## Conclusion

The New Income Tax Bill, 2025 is a significant step toward a more readable and accessible tax statute. For practitioners and taxpayers, the immediate task is to map the new provisions against the 1961 Act and ensure that the restatement has not inadvertently changed settled positions.
